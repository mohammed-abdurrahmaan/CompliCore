2 Step-by-Step User Journey Explanation

Plain-language walkthrough of every step in the workflow

Step 1: Entry into ALE Module

The user (typically an employer admin or compliance officer) logs into the system and navigates to the ALE Determination module.

At this stage, the system is preparing to evaluate whether the organization qualifies as an Applicable Large Employer (ALE) under ACA rules.

Step 2: Controlled Group Check

Before anything else, the system checks: does this employer belong to a controlled group?

A controlled group exists when multiple businesses share common ownership:

- Parent-Subsidiary: A parent company owns 80%+ of a subsidiary's stock
- Brother-Sister: The same 5 or fewer people own 80%+ of two or more companies
- Combined Group: A mix of both parent-subsidiary and brother-sister relationships

If the employer IS part of a controlled group: The system aggregates ALL employees across ALL related companies in the group. They are treated as a single employer for ALE purposes.

If standalone: Only this company's employees are counted.

This is critical. A small company with just 20 employees might think it's safe from ALE status. But if its parent company has 35 employees, the combined total is 55 — making the entire group an ALE. Every company in the controlled group shares the same ALE status.

Step 3: Selection of Measurement Year

The user selects a specific year to evaluate (e.g., 2025). This is the year whose workforce data will be analyzed.

ALE status is determined based on the previous calendar year's workforce data. So to know your ALE status for 2026, you measure 2025.

Step 4: Data Retrieval

The system pulls data for the selected year from the connected payroll system:

- Employee records (names, departments, employment types)
- Hours worked per month (for each employee, each month)
- Employment classifications (full-time, part-time, seasonal)

This data comes from payroll/HR integrations (ADP, Gusto, Paychex, etc.). The system fetches it automatically — no manual data entry needed. If the employer is part of a controlled group, data is pulled from ALL member companies.

Step 5: Data Validation

Before any calculations begin, the system checks every record for completeness:

- Are there employees with missing monthly hours?
- Are there months with no data at all?
- Are employment classifications present and correct?
- Are there any obvious anomalies (e.g., 500 hours in one month)?

If issues are found: The system stops, shows clear error messages identifying the exact problems, and prevents calculation until the data is corrected. The user fixes the issues and validation runs again.

This gatekeeping ensures accuracy before any compliance decisions are made. You cannot get an incorrect ALE result due to bad data - the system won't let you proceed.

Step 6: TRICARE Veteran Exclusion

Before any FTE calculations begin, the system identifies employees who are covered under TRICARE — the healthcare program for U.S. military service members, retirees, and their dependents.

These employees are excluded from the ALE headcount entirely. They are:

- Removed from the full-time employee count
- Removed from the part-time hours pool
- Not factored into FTE equivalents
- Logged separately with "TRICARE exclusion" as the reason

TRICARE-covered veterans already have government-provided healthcare and are not part of the employer's ACA coverage obligation. Excluding them can meaningfully reduce the FTE count — potentially enough to drop below the 50-employee ALE threshold.

Step 7: Monthly Hours Calculation

For each non-excluded employee, for each month of the selected year, the system calculates total hours worked and classifies them:

- Full-Time: 130 or more hours in the month
- Part-Time: Less than 130 hours in the month

An employee's status can change month to month. Someone might be full-time in January (135 hrs) but part-time in February (100 hrs). The system tracks this per month, not as a yearly label.

Step 8: Conversion to FTE Equivalents

Part-time employees don't count individually. Instead, their hours are pooled and converted:

Total part-time hours for the month / 120 = FTE equivalents

Example: 2 employees each working 65 hours = 130 total hours / 120 = 1.08 FTEs

The divisor is 120 (not 130). This is a specific IRS formula - 120 hours represents one full-time equivalent for the FTE pooling calculation.

Step 9: Monthly Aggregation

For each month, the system produces three numbers:

- Full-time employee count (each person who worked 130+ hours)
- FTE equivalents (from the part-time conversion above)
- Total workforce count = Full-time + FTE equivalents

This gives 12 separate workforce snapshots - one for each month of the year.

Step 10: The 12-Month Average

The system takes the total workforce count from all 12 months, adds them together, and divides by 12.

Example: If the monthly totals are 48, 49, 51, 52, 50, 47, 48, 55, 53, 50, 49, 48 → Sum = 600 → Average = 50.0

This is the single most important number in the entire process. It's the 12-month average that determines ALE status, not any single month's count.

Step 11: ALE Threshold Check

The critical decision point:

- Average >= 50: Potential ALE. But there's one more check (seasonal workers).
- Average < 50: Definitively NOT an ALE. The employer mandate does not apply. Workflow ends here.

If the average is exactly 50 (not 49.9, not 50.1, but exactly 50.0), the employer IS a potential ALE. The threshold is "50 or more."

Step 12: Seasonal Worker Exception

Even if the 12-month average is 50 or above, the system checks one final escape route:

Were the excess employees (above 50) seasonal workers employed for 120 days or fewer?

- YES: The employer is NOT an ALE. The seasonal worker exception applies. The excess was temporary and doesn't count.
- NO: The employer IS an ALE. The excess was from regular (non-seasonal) employees.

This exception exists because some industries (agriculture, retail, tourism) have predictable seasonal spikes. The IRS doesn't penalize employers for temporary, seasonal workforce increases.

Step 13: ALE Status Assignment & Storage

If the employer meets the criteria (average >= 50, no seasonal exception), they are officially marked as an ALE in the system.

The system stores: ALE status, the year it applies to, the 12-month average, and all monthly calculation details for audit purposes.

This stored status is used across every other compliance module. MEC checks, affordability tests, and IRS reporting all reference this ALE determination.

Step 14: Downstream Module Triggers

Once marked as ALE, the system automatically flags the next compliance steps:

- MEC Validation: Is the employer offering coverage to 95%+ of full-time employees?
- Affordability Analysis: Is the employee's share of premium below 9.02% of income?
- IRS Reporting: Generate data for Form 1094-C (transmittal) and Form 1095-C (per employee)

These modules cannot run meaningfully without knowing ALE status first. ALE determination is the gateway to the entire compliance workflow.

Step 15: Dashboard Display

The user sees a summary dashboard showing:

- ALE status (Yes / No)
- The 12-month average number
- Controlled group details (if applicable — member companies, individual vs combined counts)
- Monthly breakdown table (FT count, FTE equivalents, total per month)
- TRICARE exclusions summary (count of excluded veterans)
- Seasonal worker analysis (if applicable)
- Compliance risk indicators
- Links to triggered downstream modules

This is the final output - a clear, auditable summary the compliance officer can review, export, or share with stakeholders.

Three Types of Controlled Groups

TypeDefinitionExampleOwnership Threshold
Parent-Subsidiary One company (parent) owns 80% or more of another company (subsidiary). Can chain: parent → child → grandchild. Acme Corp owns 85% of Acme Logistics and 90% of Acme Tech. All three are one controlled group. 80% stock ownership
Brother-Sister The same 5 or fewer individuals own 80%+ of two or more companies, with each person owning at least some stock in each. Jane owns 80% of a restaurant and 80% of a retail store. Both are one controlled group. 80% common ownership by same 5 or fewer people
Combined Group A group that includes both parent-subsidiary and brother-sister relationships among its members. A parent-subsidiary chain where one of the subsidiaries also has a brother-sister relationship with another company. Combination of both rules
Why This Matters — A Common Trap:
Many business owners think each of their companies is evaluated separately. A restaurant with 20 employees seems far from the 50-employee ALE threshold. But if the same owner also runs a catering company (18 employees) and a food truck business (15 employees), the combined total is 53 employees — making ALL THREE businesses ALEs that must offer health coverage.
How the System Handles It:
1. During onboarding, the employer declares related companies and ownership percentages
2. The system identifies if the 80% threshold is met for any controlled group type
3. Employee data is automatically pulled from all member companies' payroll systems
4. The merged roster is used for ALE calculation — individual company counts are also shown for reference
5. If the group is determined to be an ALE, the status is applied to every member company

120 Days, Not 120 Hours:
The seasonal worker exception uses 120 calendar days (not hours). If seasonal workers were employed for more than 120 days, even if they only worked a few hours per week, the exception does NOT apply. The count is about duration of employment, not hours worked.
What Counts as "Seasonal"?
The IRS doesn't have a rigid definition. Generally, it's work that is performed during a certain part of the year due to the nature of the work (e.g., harvest, ski season, summer tourism). It doesn't include "busy season" for year-round businesses or permanent employees hired during a busy period.

$2,900/yr

Per full-time employee (minus first 30). Triggered when employer fails to offer coverage to 95%+ of FT employees.

$4,350/yr

Per affected employee. Triggered when coverage is offered but unaffordable or below MV, and employee gets marketplace subsidy.

Not ALE - No Penalties

$0

No employer mandate. No penalty exposure. Coverage is optional but encouraged.

Why the system blocks calculation on bad data:
An incorrect ALE determination can lead to two costly outcomes: (1) the employer wrongly believes they're not an ALE and fails to offer coverage, resulting in IRS penalties, or (2) the employer wrongly believes they're an ALE and spends money on coverage they weren't required to provide. Both are prevented by strict data validation.

Summary - The ALE Determination in One Sentence:
First aggregate employees across all companies in a controlled group (80%+ common ownership), exclude TRICARE-covered veterans, then for each of the 12 months count full-time employees (130+ hrs/month) and add part-time FTE equivalents (total PT hours / 120), average those 12 monthly totals, and if the result is 50 or more (with no seasonal worker exception), the entire group is an ALE and every member company must offer affordable, qualifying health coverage or face IRS penalties.
